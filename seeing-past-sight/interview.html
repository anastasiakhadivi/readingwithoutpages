an interview with

Charles Catherine

Charles Catherine is a visually impaired runner, lawyer, and advocate based in NYC. He currently works for a foundation, achilles, merging the world of athletes and those with disabilities.

Adam Rosenberg: What do you find to be the most difficult aspect of being apart of the visually impaired community - for both yourself and those you work with?

Charles Catherine: The pity that others place upon us. People are quick to judge what we can and can’t do and often times, all we need to be included is a quick reimagining of the typical or standard methods.

Adam Rosenberg: Is there one thing that you repeatedly hear from those without visual impairments? Are there things you think they’re afraid to say?

Charles Catherine: Haha, I find that people are always concerned after they say the word “see”… “I’m seeing this new woman”, “I saw a great movie last weekend”. I can feel them tense up after they use the word. They really shouldn’t be bothered by this, but us in the VIP community always laugh about it. I’ve also found them to not know how to discuss any form of literature - books, newspaper articles, etc, because of the issue of reading. I’m sure they wonder how we do it if we don’t all rely on braille.

Adam Rosenberg: So what’s the secret? How would you go about reading the NYTimes everyday?

Charles Catherine: Technology. Audio books, screen readers, Siri, and high contrast large type for some. Although they don’t always have the best voices, I can listen to an article if I want to. There is this one dial in service that will read you an array of articles from all different publications if you call in, but it’s always the full article. Getting shorter briefs is actually something that my good friend Alexa has started to help with - it’s bad enough to feel signaled out with fully sighted persons, I always like to know what’s going on in the world so bridge the gap between sighted and unsighted. There’s really nothing different between us, but I know it can be awkward at first.
